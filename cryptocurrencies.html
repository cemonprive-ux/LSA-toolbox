Cryptocurrencies in a few words

A financial revolution under scrutiny: opportunities, risks, and the question of who we trust with money.

Over the past decade, cryptocurrencies have moved from the fringes of the internet to the centre of global financial debate. Bitcoin, Ethereum, and thousands of other digital assets now represent a multi-trillion-dollar market, attracting both enthusiastic investors and deep institutional concern. Understanding what is at stake — economically, socially, and politically — is essential for any observer of contemporary finance.

IA new monetary paradigm

1 · The foundations of moneyA challenge to central banks

At their core, cryptocurrencies challenge the very foundations of traditional money. Unlike conventional currencies issued by central banks, they rely on blockchain technology: a decentralised, tamper-proof ledger that records transactions without any intermediary. This design was originally driven by a desire to bypass banks and governments, giving individuals direct control over their assets.

2 · For their supportersA genuine breakthrough

For their supporters, this represents a genuine breakthrough. In countries where banking systems are unreliable or where inflation erodes savings rapidly — as seen in Argentina or Venezuela — cryptocurrencies offer an alternative store of value. They also facilitate cross-border transfers at lower costs and greater speed than conventional wire transfers, which could benefit migrant workers sending remittances to their families.

IIEconomic opportunities and risks

1 · The promiseReturns and decentralised finance

From an investment perspective, cryptocurrencies have generated extraordinary returns for early adopters, fuelling widespread interest. Decentralised Finance (DeFi) platforms now allow users to lend, borrow, and earn interest without involving any bank, representing a significant shift in how financial services might be structured in the future.

2 · The other sideVolatility and collapse

However, these opportunities come with serious risks. Price volatility remains extreme: Bitcoin lost over 70% of its value in 2022 alone, wiping out savings for millions of retail investors. Unlike bank deposits, crypto holdings are not covered by any deposit guarantee scheme. When platforms collapse — as FTX dramatically did in 2022 — investors have little legal recourse.

An energy concern

The energy consumption of proof-of-work cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin is staggering. According to various estimates, Bitcoin mining consumes more electricity annually than several medium-sized countries, raising pressing environmental concerns at a time when carbon emissions must be reduced urgently.

IIIRegulatory and political stakes

1 · A difficult positionOutside the rules

Governments and central banks find themselves in a difficult position. Cryptocurrencies operate largely outside existing regulatory frameworks, making them attractive tools for money laundering, tax evasion, and sanctions circumvention. The anonymity they provide — though often overstated — complicates law enforcement efforts worldwide.

2 · The responseRegulators push back

Regulators are now pushing back. The European Union's MiCA regulation (Markets in Crypto-Assets), adopted in 2023, represents the most comprehensive attempt so far to bring crypto markets under a coherent legal framework. In the United States, a fierce debate continues over whether crypto assets should be classified as securities or commodities, with significant consequences for oversight.

3 · The state's answerThe rise of CBDCs

In response to the rise of private cryptocurrencies, many central banks are exploring their own digital currencies, known as CBDCs (Central Bank Digital Currencies). The digital euro and the digital yuan are both in advanced stages of development. These state-backed alternatives would preserve monetary sovereignty while offering some of the efficiency gains associated with digital payments.

A question of trust

Ultimately, the cryptocurrency debate is about trust — who controls money, who guarantees its value, and who bears the risks when things go wrong. Advocates argue that decentralisation empowers individuals and reduces the power of institutions that have repeatedly failed ordinary citizens. Critics point out that in the absence of regulation, ordinary investors and vulnerable populations often suffer the most.

The future of cryptocurrencies will likely be shaped by how effectively regulators manage to balance innovation with protection, and by whether digital assets can eventually deliver on their most ambitious promises: greater financial inclusion, reduced transaction costs, and a more resilient global financial system.
