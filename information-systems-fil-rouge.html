Opportunity or threat? The technology debate that returns every year

No theme appears more often than technology, even when it is hidden inside other subjects. Artificial intelligence reaches into marketing, human resources, governance and the workplace, so the information-systems questions are really the centre of the whole exam. Yet behind every wording lies the same approach: new technologies, and AI in particular, bring huge opportunities and serious risks, and the real issue is how to manage the change responsibly. A candidate who masters this single debate is ready for many different questions.

The first recurring angle is opportunity, especially productivity. The 2023 text on ChatGPT and the 2024 text on a possible four-day week both show how technology can help us do more in less time. The key knowledge is concrete: generative AI can write, summarise, translate and code; automation can take over repetitive tasks; data tools can speed up decisions. A reusable argument is that, used well, technology acts like a tireless assistant that frees people for creative and human work.

The second recurring angle is the threat to jobs and the change in skills. If machines can do tasks once reserved for humans, some jobs will disappear or change deeply. The useful position, which works across years, is that the likely result is not the end of work but a major shift in the skills companies need. Employees will have to learn to work alongside AI, checking its output and adding the human understanding that machines lack. This is why upskilling and reskilling are central ideas.

The third recurring angle is the set of risks that come with the technology. Candidates should be able to name them clearly: bias, when an algorithm reproduces unfair patterns hidden in its data; hallucination, when AI produces confident but false answers; privacy, when employees feed confidential data into online tools; and cybersecurity, when the same technology helps criminals create convincing scams. The reusable argument is that powerful tools demand strong safeguards and constant human supervision.

The fourth recurring angle is governance and regulation. The debate has clearly moved from what AI can do to how it should be controlled. The key references are the European AI Act, which sets rules according to the level of risk, and the GDPR, which protects personal data. The useful idea is that data governance, clear internal policies and a human in the loop are now part of any serious technology strategy, and that ignoring regulation can lead to fines or forced withdrawal of a system.

A fifth, more human angle often appears: who benefits from the gains? If AI saves time, the time can go to employees as more rest, as in the four-day-week scenario, or only to shareholders as higher profit. The reusable point is that the outcome depends on human choices, not on the technology itself, and that companies which share the benefits fairly keep their staff motivated.

What ties all of this together is the classic opportunity-versus-threat structure. A strong answer never says simply that AI is good or bad; it shows the benefits, then the risks, then explains how good management and sensible regulation can capture the benefits while limiting the dangers. This balanced approach fits every technology question, whatever its exact wording.

L'essentiel à retenir

A few reflexes are valuable. Define the technology in simple terms. Give one concrete example of a benefit and one of a risk. Mention people: which jobs and skills are affected. Mention rules: the AI Act, the GDPR, the need for human oversight. And conclude that technology should serve people rather than replace human judgement. With this base, the information-systems theme, and all the AI questions hidden in other themes, become far easier to handle.
